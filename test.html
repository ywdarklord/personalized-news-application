Every child must be told why games kick off seven minutes late this weekend

By Jamie Carragher

Published: 16:13 EST, 11 April 2014 | Updated: 20:48 EST, 11 April 2014

788 shares

0

View
comments

Hillsborough. Say the word to those  of a certain age on Merseyside and almost all will be able to tell you where they were, and what they were doing, on April 15, 1989.

I was at Villa Park on what turned out to be the darkest day in English football’s history. Everton were playing Norwich in the FA Cup semi-final, Pat Nevin scored the winning goal and it should have been one of the great days of my young life.

Instead it was anything but. I remember an announcement being made at half-time to say the other semi-final between Liverpool  and Nottingham Forest had been abandoned but as these were the days before mobile phones and social media nobody knew why.

It was only when I got into the car with my dad that we began to learn of the horrors that unfolded 70 miles away in Sheffield. We travelled back from Birmingham in near silence, worrying about my cousin, Jamie, and all the other Liverpool fans who we knew had gone to Hillsborough.

Only when we got back did we hear the full extent. I was only 11 then and my final memory of the day was when the BBC announced Match of the Day had been cancelled out of respect to those who had died.

Everyone we knew, thankfully, came home. For the families of the 96, however, the nightmare was just beginning and it is a national  disgrace that they were made to  suffer for so long.

Those families have fought tirelessly for justice over the past 25 years and at Anfield tomorrow, before Liverpool’s showdown with Manchester City, then again on Tuesday, when the annual Memorial Service is staged, we will pay tribute to the ones who so tragically passed away.

Across Stanley Park, Everton will do the same, as pictures of the service will be beamed on to a big screen at Goodison Park; Everton have stood shoulder-to-shoulder with Liverpool over Hillsborough, which is why  you should never class it as a one-football-club tragedy.

It wasn’t. It affected the whole city, as Evertonians had family, friends and work colleagues at Hillsborough. The rivalry between the clubs is intense but, for this particular matter, the bond is unbreakable.

When I started attending the Memorial Service as a Liverpool player in 1996, I understood what had happened, but it was only after I became a father in 2002 that I really got a sense of perspective on the agony those parents who lost sons and daughters must have endured.

At times during those services, when I heard the congregation singing for ‘justice’, I lost hope that they would ever get a resolution but their resolve, courage and determination to keep fighting has been inspirational, not just to everyone at the club but around the city and far beyond.

Leader: Liverpool manager Kenny Dalglish sees the horror of the Hillsborough disaster first hand

Memorial: Scarves, shirts and the plaque at the Shankly Gates at Anfield marking the 20th anniversary in 2009

Unbreakable bond: An Everton and Liverpool fan stand together on Goodison Park in tribute to the 96 in 2012

The families of the 96 got their  breakthrough in 2009, at the 20th Anniversary Memorial Service.

Andy Burnham, who was then the Secretary of State for Culture, Media and Sport, addressed the crowd but he was shouted down.  Afterwards, I attended a reception at Liverpool Town Hall with Steven Gerrard and Kenny Dalglish as the families were being honoured with the Freedom of the City; Burnham was visibly shaken by what had happened and he said that night: ‘We will get to the bottom of this.’

Hopefully, now that the fresh inquests into the deaths have started, some form of closure will arrive at some point in the near future, but that does not mean what happened that day in Sheffield can ever be forgotten.

Liverpool Ambassador: Club legend Dalglish went to all 96 funerals and even attended four in one day

Respect: The clock at Hillsborough is stopped at 15.06, the time the game was stopped (left), while Sheffield Wednesday have paid tribute on the cover of the match programme for Saturday's game with Blackburn (right)

Never forget: Liverpool fans show their respect to the people who died before a home game at Anfield

Speech: Andy Burham insisted 'we will get to the bottom of this' after speaking at the 2009 memorial service

Up and down the country this weekend, young children will be going to games and asking their  parents why all matches are kicking off seven minutes later than they are normally scheduled, after a period of silence. It is important they, and future generations, understand what happened. No stadium will carry the feeling like Anfield; Liverpool’s players will have to use the emotion to their advantage and not let it affect them the way it happened to Manchester United when they marked the 50th anniversary of the Munich Air Disaster against Manchester City.

Anfield is unique when it comes to generating atmosphere on special occasions. As a player, you try to block the noise out when the game begins but you are always conscious of the impact it can have and  tomorrow could be a day to rival St Etienne in 1977 or Chelsea in 2005.

I played in the latter game, the Champions League semi-final, and it was the most intense atmosphere I experienced. When Liverpool’s fans build up a head of steam, they can influence the result of a game and that certainly happened against Chelsea nine years ago.

Super sub: David Fairclough scores for Liverpool against St Etienne in the 1977 European Cup quarter-final

Famous night: Liverpool frontman Luis Garcia (second right) celebrates after scoring against Chelsea in 2005

Team effort: Carragher punches the air and celebrates with goalkeeper Jerzy Dudek after beating Chelsea

There is a month of the campaign to go but it feels like the title decider. A draw would let Chelsea back in, assuming they beat Swansea, but the way Liverpool and City are playing, there should be goals. Liverpool need three points. City, with a game in hand, would be the happier if it is a stalemate.

Incidentally, the last time Liverpool were involved in a title race, in 2009, it came in the year of a significant Hillsborough anniversary.

We finished second that season, despite our best efforts. It would be fitting, then, if Brendan Rodgers and his squad could go one step  better this time.

Runners-up: Steven Gerrard celebrates scoring against Man United when Liverpool finished second in 2009

 

TOP FIVE... YOUNG PLAYERS OF THE YEAR

Luis Suarez is odds-on to win PFA Player of the Year but less clear-cut is who will receive the Young Player’s award.

Eden Hazard and Daniel Sturridge are the favourites but these five young stars also deserve to be high up in people’s thinking.

Clear favourite: Liverpool frontman Luis Suarez is odds-on to win the PFA Player of the Year award

Boy in Blue: Chelsea wideman Eden Hazard has been one of the standout performers for the Blues this season

Natural goalscorer: Liverpool striker Daniel Sturridge has been lethal in front of the goal for the Reds

 

1. Raheem Sterling

There was always a buzz around Raheem when I was at Liverpool but this season he has shown there is substance to the hype.

He has been brilliant since getting back into the team, adding goals to his speed on the flanks, and he looks set to go to the World Cup.

Living up to the hype: Liverpool frontman Raheem Sterling has added goals to his game this season

 

2. Ross Barkley

Back in August, when I wrote my second column for Sportsmail, I highlighted the Everton midfielder as one to watch and he has done nothing but improve.

An explosive talent, with energy and two great feet, he is another who Roy Hodgson will come calling for in June.

One to watch: Ross Barkley has constantly improved this season as Everton challenge for fourth

 

3. Luke Shaw

Scouts have been flocking to St Mary’s to see Shaw in action and it is no wonder the top clubs want to prise the left back away from Southampton.

If he moves this summer, Shaw will command a huge fee but it will be warranted as he has consistently impressed.

Top talent: Southampton's Luke Shaw has attracted attention from a host of rival Premier League clubs

 

4. Adnan Januzaj

Manchester United have endured a disappointing campaign but the one bright spot has been the emergence of Januzaj.

He’s played 32 games, which is a lot for a  19-year-old, but he has a big future. I liked what I saw of him in pre-season and he has built on that impression.

Bright spot: Adnan Januzaj has shined in an otherwise disappointing campaign for Manchester United

 

5. John Stones

Roberto Martinez is not afraid to give young players opportunities if they are good enough and Stones has impressed since being given his Everton chance.

I like the way he uses the ball and it was interesting to see he completed 39 of the 40 passes he attempted against Arsenal last week.

Taking his chances: Everton defender John Stones (left) is equally adept at passing as he his tackling

 

Hull manager Steve Bruce deserves more credit

There has been a lot of talk about who should be crowned Manager of the Year and the vote seems to be split between Brendan Rodgers and Roberto Martinez.

Tony Pulis, of course, is also in the running thanks to the outstanding job he has done with Crystal Palace, but why has there not been more made of Steve Bruce’s work at Hull?

hen Hull came up last season, many would have had them in their three picks for relegation but it looks like they are going to stay in the Barclays Premier League quite comfortably and they could even crown the campaign with success in the FA Cup.

Under the radar: Steve Bruce has guided Hull to mid-table and could crown the season with Cup success

Bruce, who wants his teams to play in the right style and spirit, deserves to lead Hull out against Sheffield United at Wembley tomorrow and I doubt anyone would begrudge him if he returned next month and climbed the 107 steps to get the trophy, 20 years after he did so as Manchester United’s captain.

It used to be said he would be a contender to take over the reins from Sir Alex Ferguson one day but there is a chance an even bigger job might come on his radar in the future, particularly if he puts silverware on his CV.

Should the FA keep with their current mantra of wanting an Englishman to manage the England team, I’m sure Bruce would come into their thoughts.

He turned down the chance to win an England cap when he was in his 30s but there is little chance he would turn down the FA a second time.

Captain fantastic: Bruce (left) holds aloft the FA Cup in 1994 following Man United's final win over Chelsea

SPORTSMAIL HALL OF FAME

  • Roy Keane: He's always spoken his mind but on the pitch his feet did the talking - this all-action hero could do everything (including taming Vieira)
  • Zinedine Zidane: He was God, the King, the Master, a man for the big occasion - however, there'll always be a butt
  • Jackie Joyner-Kersee: Heptathlon's original Golden Girl who became the First Lady of track and field
  • Steffi Graf: Germany's golden girl was queen of the court and the greatest sportswoman who ever lived
  • Ronaldo: The assassin who made you smile as he ruled the world and scored goals for fun
  • Sam Tomkins: The Superman of Super League who lifted an entire sport
  • Sir Steve Redgrave: The British Bulldog who refused to lose and who set the gold standard for our Olympians
  • George Best: Confident, fast, fearless, the most gifted star to grace British football
  • Michael Johnson: A record-breaker who owned the track and was so special he could even pull off those golden spikes
  • Diego Maradona: He had the Hand of God and a divine left foot and was perhaps the greatest footballer of all-time
  • Richie McCaw: The All Blacks' most decorated captain who is one of the true rugby greats
  • Desert Orchid: Glorious grey who won the hearts of the nation
  • Ayrton Senna: Master of the track who lived life on the edge and whose shadow still hangs over F1
  • Tiger Woods by Derek Lawrenson... the man who ripped up the record books to elevate golf to new heights
  • Muhammad Ali by Jeff Powell... The world's greatest in and out of the ring
  • Seb Coe by Matt Lawton... from Olympics glory to running London 2012, why he's Britain's Golden Boy
  • Graham Gooch by Nasser Hussain... he was the best cricketer I ever played with
  • Roger Federer by Mike Dickson... the tennis legend is the first into our Hall of Fame
